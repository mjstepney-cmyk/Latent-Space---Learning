Chapter 01 / 10

What AI actually is

Strip away the hype and "artificial intelligence" describes something surprisingly specific: software that learns its own rules from examples, instead of having every rule written by hand.

Two ways to make a machine behave

For most of computing history, software worked one way: a human anticipated every situation and wrote an explicit instruction for it. If the temperature exceeds 25°C, turn on the fan. This is powerful, but brittle — it only handles situations someone thought to write down.

Machine learning flips the relationship. Instead of writing rules, you provide examples of inputs paired with the right outputs — thousands of emails labelled "spam" or "not spam" — and the machine works out the rules itself. The "rules" it discovers are stored as numbers, which we'll meet properly in Chapter 4.

Mental model

Recipe vs. apprentice

Two ways to get a kitchen to produce a great curry:

  • Traditional software is a recipe. Every step specified in advance. Follow it exactly, get the same dish — but it can't handle a missing ingredient or a new request.
  • Machine learning is an apprentice. You don't hand it instructions — you let it taste a thousand curries, attempt its own, and adjust after every failure. Eventually it can improvise dishes nobody wrote down. But you can't fully open its head and read off why it added the lime.

That last point — capability without full explainability — is the source of both AI's power and most of its governance challenges.

Where today's AI sits

You'll hear three nested terms. Artificial intelligence is the broad ambition: machines doing things that seem to require intelligence. Machine learning is the dominant method: learning from data rather than hand-written rules. Deep learning is the technique behind nearly every recent breakthrough: machine learning using large, layered neural networks — the subject of the chapters ahead.

Key takeaway

AI isn't magic or consciousness — it's software whose behaviour is learned from examples rather than programmed. Everything else in this journey follows from that one shift.

Chapter 02 / 10

Seventy years in seven moments

AI's history isn't a smooth ascent. It's a cycle of bold promises and hard "winters", with one decisive turning point: the 2017 transformer. The core ideas are old — what changed recently is scale.

Why did it take so long?

The core ideas behind today's AI — neural networks, learning from data — date to the 1940s–60s. What was missing for half a century was not insight but scale: enough computing power, enough digital data, and an architecture able to exploit both. All three arrived together in the 2010s, and progress that had crawled for decades suddenly compounded.

Chapter 03 / 10

How machines learn

"Learning" sounds mysterious. It isn't. It is a simple loop: guess → measure the error → adjust → repeat, performed millions of times at machine speed.

Suppose you want a model to predict house prices from floor area. The data is a cloud of dots. The model's job is to find the line through them that makes the smallest mistakes. Try it yourself first — then watch the machine do it.

Interactive · try it

Each dot is a house — floor area along the bottom, price up the side. The model's whole job is to draw the straight line that sits as close as possible to every dot. The orange stalks show how wrong it currently is.

Why this mattersThis is machine learning stripped to its bones. Your line has just two adjustable numbers — a slope and a height. "Training" means nudging those numbers until the total error stops shrinking. A frontier AI runs this identical downhill-nudging process — only with a trillion numbers instead of two. What you just watched

That automatic process is gradient descent — the algorithm at the heart of nearly all modern AI. At every step the system asks one question: "if I nudge each of my numbers slightly, which direction reduces my error?" Then it nudges, re-measures, and repeats. The measurement of error is called the loss; training is simply the relentless minimisation of loss.

Your demo had two adjustable numbers. A frontier language model runs exactly the same loop — with over a trillion of them, adjusted across trillions of examples. The principle does not change. Only the scale does.

Key takeaway

Training = guess, measure error, nudge every number downhill, repeat. The two sliders you just moved are, at miniature scale, exactly what "parameters" are — which brings us to the most important concept in this journey.

Chapter 04 / 10

Parameters: the knobs

When you hear "GPT-4 has over a trillion parameters", here is what that means: a trillion adjustable numbers, each one a tiny dial that training has tuned. Together, they are the model.

A parameter is a number that decides how much weight to give a piece of evidence. A neural network is nothing more than millions of these weighted judgements stacked on top of each other. Everything the model "knows" — grammar, facts, reasoning patterns, tone — is stored not as text or rules, but as the precise settings of these numbers.

Chapter 05 / 10

Language as prediction

A large language model does one thing, repeatedly: predict the next word. Everything that looks like writing, reasoning or conversation emerges from that single trick performed extraordinarily well.

First, machines don't see words

Before any prediction happens, text is chopped into tokens — chunks of roughly word-or-part-of-word size, each mapped to a number. This is why models sometimes struggle to count letters in a word: they never see letters, only token IDs.

Interactive · type anything

The tokeniser

Type a sentence. Before a model can do anything with text, it chops it into tokens — chunks of roughly word-or-part-of-word size — and turns each into a number. Watch your words split as you type.

Why this mattersThis is the model's eyes: it never sees letters, only these numbered chunks. That single fact explains a lot of real behaviour downstream — why models can stumble on counting the letters in a word, why long inputs cost more, and what "context window" really measures. (Simplified here — real tokenisers learn their splits statistically — but the idea is identical.)

Then: predict, pick, repeat

Given the tokens so far, the model outputs a probability for every possible next token — tens of thousands of them. One is chosen, appended, and the process repeats. That's it. That's how every essay, answer and poem is generated: one weighted guess at a time.

Interactive · build a sentence

Next-token roulette

At every step a language model produces odds for what word should come next. Below are live probabilities for continuing the sentence. Click a word to choose it, or let the model sample one for you.

Why this mattersThis loop — predict the next token, pick one, repeat — is the entire mechanism behind every essay, answer and line of code a model produces. The temperature dial controls how adventurously it picks: low is cautious and repetitive, high is creative and risky. And it's the same mechanism that explains hallucination — the model optimises for plausible, not true, so when those diverge you get a confident, fluent fabrication.
Watch the bars as you change temperature: low sharpens the odds toward the favourite; high flattens them so surprises become likely. Probabilities here are illustrative, to show the mechanism.

Two big implications follow. First, output is probabilistic — ask twice, get different answers; that's a feature, governed by temperature. Second, the model optimises for plausible, not true. When plausibility and truth diverge, you get a confident fabrication — a hallucination. It isn't lying; it's doing exactly what it was trained to do.

Key takeaway

LLMs convert text to tokens and predict the next one, over and over. Fluency, apparent reasoning — and hallucination — are all downstream consequences of that single objective.

Chapter 06 / 10

The transformer moment

In 2017, eight Google researchers published "Attention Is All You Need." The architecture it introduced — the transformer — is the "T" in GPT and the engine of every frontier model since.

The problem it solved

Earlier language models read text like a narrow torch beam — one word at a time, left to right, with fading memory of what came before. By the end of a long sentence, the beginning had blurred. The transformer's breakthrough, attention, lets every word look directly at every other word at once and decide which ones matter for its meaning.

"The animal didn't cross the street because it was too tired" — it must mean the animal. Change tired to wide and it must mean the street. No rule was written for this. Attention is computed in parallel across the whole text, which also made transformers dramatically faster to train on modern chips — and that efficiency is what unlocked the era of scale.

Why this changed everything

  • Long-range understanding. A word on page 40 can directly inform a word on page 1's interpretation.
  • Parallelism. Whole documents processed at once, not word-by-word — perfect for GPUs, so models could grow 10,000× in five years.
  • Generality. The same architecture turned out to work for images, audio, code and protein structures. One idea, every domain.
Key takeaway

Attention lets every token weigh every other token's relevance, in parallel. That single mechanism gave models deep context and made them efficient enough to scale — the two ingredients of the modern AI boom.

Chapter 07 / 10

The modern era — and what to do with it

You now hold the full chain: examples → loss → gradient descent → parameters → tokens → attention → scale. This final chapter assembles it into the systems making headlines, and is honest about their limits.

How a frontier model is actually built

STAGE 1

Pre-training

Predict the next token across trillions of words from the internet, books and code. Months of compute, costs in the hundreds of millions. Produces raw capability — knowledgeable but unguided.

→
STAGE 2

Fine-tuning

Further training on curated examples of high-quality, helpful behaviour — teaching the raw predictor to act like an assistant.

→
STAGE 3

Human feedback

People rank competing answers; the model is tuned toward preferred ones (RLHF and successors). This is where helpfulness, tone and refusals are shaped.

What they're genuinely good and bad at

  • Strong: drafting, summarising, translating, coding, restructuring information, brainstorming, explaining — anything where fluent pattern-completion over vast prior text is the job.
  • Weak: guaranteed factual accuracy (hallucination is inherent to next-token prediction), knowledge after their training cut-off, precise arithmetic, and consistency — the same question can yield different answers.
  • Inherited bias: models learn the patterns of their data, flaws included. Mitigation is active, not automatic.
  • Opacity: a trillion tuned numbers can't be read like source code. "Interpretability" research is racing to change that — it matters enormously for high-stakes deployment.

One more shift — and it's already here

Scaling has not yet hit a wall. But the bigger shift happening right now isn't models getting larger — it's models getting agency. Rather than answering questions, they're beginning to take actions: browsing, writing code, sending emails, making decisions across long sequences of steps. That transition is the subject of the final chapter.

Key takeaway

You can now decode almost any AI headline: parameters are tuned dials, training is error-minimisation at scale, attention is context, and limits follow from the objective. That mental model is the literacy. Chapter 8 applies it to where the field is right now.

Chapter 08 / 10

AI that acts: the agentic era

Until recently, AI answered. Now it is beginning to do. The same generative models you've learned about are being wrapped in systems that plan, use tools, and execute tasks across minutes or hours — with limited human involvement.

From adviser to executor

The distinction matters. When you ask a model a question and read the answer, you are in control: you evaluate it, decide what to do, and act. An agentic system collapses that gap. Given a goal, it breaks the task into steps, calls tools (web search, code execution, email, databases), checks its own progress, and loops until done — often without a human seeing each step.

The analogy from the leave-behind still holds, but it shifts: this is no longer the brilliant intern who hands you a draft. It's the intern who has been given a key to the filing cabinet, access to the email system, and told to handle it.

The building blocks: what makes a system agentic

  • Tool use. The model can call external functions — search, read a file, run code, send a message. It decides when and which tool to use.
  • Memory. State is preserved across steps: what has been done, what was found, what remains. Without this, each step is blind to the last.
  • Orchestration. A layer coordinates the loop: passing the model's output to the right tool, feeding results back in, deciding when the task is done.
  • Autonomous loops. The agent self-evaluates: "Have I answered the goal? If not, what next?" It loops until satisfied — or until it hits a limit or error.

Where the major systems stand — June 2026

All major AI platforms have moved in this direction simultaneously. The question is no longer whether to engage with agentic AI but how — because it is now a standard capability, not a research preview.

Key takeaway

Agentic AI doesn't change the underlying technology — the model is still the next-token predictor from Chapter 5. What changes is its relationship with the world: it now plans, uses tools and acts across many steps before a human sees anything. The final chapter takes that fact into the workplace — because it changes far more than the technology.

Chapter 09 / 10

What changes for leaders

Agentic AI is only half a technology challenge. The harder half is governance and people: when systems decide and act on your behalf, the controls, skills and accountability your organisation relies on all need redesigning — and the human role in decisions can quietly shrink to a formality unless you choose otherwise.

The immediate shifts

  • Oversight moves upstream. You can no longer just review the output — you need to govern the process. Which tools can the agent call? What can it send externally? What triggers a human checkpoint?
  • Error compounds. In a single-shot answer, a mistake stays in the answer. In an agentic loop, a wrong assumption in step 2 propagates through steps 3, 4 and 5 before anyone notices.
  • Audit trails matter. If an agent took an action — filed something, sent a communication, updated a record — who is accountable, and is there a log? This is an unsolved governance problem in most organisations.
  • Speed creates pressure. Agents complete in minutes what would take a person hours. That efficiency is real — and it compresses the time available for human judgement.

Where this is heading: the human role shrinks

Follow the logic forward and an uncomfortable picture emerges. As agents become more reliable, the economic pressure is always the same — let them handle more, and have humans touch less. Across many business processes, the human role is migrating from doing the work, to reviewing the work, to occasionally auditing a sample of the work, to — in some cases — being notified that work happened.

This isn't speculation about distant AGI; it's the near-term trajectory of processes that are already being automated.

Why this mattersEach step rightward is individually rational — the agent is faster and, eventually, more consistent. But the cumulative effect is that meaningful human judgement quietly exits the process. By the final stage, no person has genuinely decided anything; they've been informed. The danger isn't a single bad call — it's that the capacity to make the call, and the institutional knowledge behind it, atrophies.

What "marginal" actually looks like

The concern isn't that humans vanish — it's that human judgement becomes a formality rather than a force. Three failure modes worth naming for any leadership team:

  • Rubber-stamping. When an agent is right 99% of the time, reviewers stop reviewing properly. The approval becomes a reflex, and the 1% sails through unexamined — often the very cases that most needed a human.
  • Skill erosion. If agents handle all the routine cases, people lose the fluency that let them judge the hard ones. The expertise needed to oversee the system is exactly the expertise the system stops you practising.
  • Accountability without authority. A named human remains "accountable" on paper, but has neither the time nor the information to meaningfully intervene. They carry the risk without holding the controls — the worst of both worlds.

Why agentic AI breaks the governance model

Most organisational governance was designed around a quiet assumption: that somewhere in every process there is a human, a decision, and a reviewable artefact. Agentic systems dissolve all three. The controls we trust were built for a world that agents don't operate in — which is why this is not a problem you can buy your way out of with better technology.

It's a people and governance problem, not just a tech one

This is the heart of it. Organisations instinctively treat AI adoption as a technology programme — procure the tool, integrate it, train people to use it. But the failures of agentic AI mostly come from the other two dimensions. Success depends on three pillars, and the technology is the one most organisations over-weight.

The people capability gap

"Upskilling for AI" is usually pitched as teaching staff to use the tools. The harder, more important capability is teaching an organisation to oversee systems that act on its behalf — a genuinely new competence that most workforces don't yet have. It spans new skills, new roles and a cultural shift:

  • Oversight literacy. Knowing how to supervise an agent you can't fully see inside — what to sample, what to question, when an "efficient" result should still feel wrong. This is a skill, and it must be practised.
  • New roles. Agent supervisors, assurance and audit specialists, escalation designers, "human-in-the-loop" architects. Roles defined by judgement and accountability, not task execution.
  • Preserved expertise. Deliberately keeping enough people doing enough of the real work to retain the judgement needed to oversee the machines — resisting the efficiency pull to automate that away.
  • A culture that challenges. Staff confident enough to override a confident agent, and a leadership that rewards the override that prevents harm rather than only the throughput the agent delivers.

The leadership choice — not the technology's

Here is the part that matters most: how marginal the human becomes is a design decision, not an inevitability. The technology enables a spectrum; where on it your organisation lands is chosen — explicitly or by default. The leaders who do this well will deliberately decide which judgements stay human because they should, not because the agent couldn't technically handle them. Some decisions warrant a person not for accuracy, but for legitimacy, accountability and public trust — and that is a values question, not a capability one.

Practically, that means designing processes around questions like: Which decisions must a human genuinely make, not merely approve? Where do we deliberately keep a person in the loop even at a cost to speed? How do we preserve the skills needed to oversee the system? Key takeaway

Agentic AI is only half a technology challenge. The harder half is governance and people — redesigning controls for actors that decide and act on their own, and building a workforce that can oversee them. Treat it as a tech rollout and it will quietly hollow out your decision-making. Treat it as an organisational-design and capability challenge — deciding which judgements stay human and protecting them on purpose — and it becomes a genuine advantage.

What you now know

Nine chapters, one coherent picture. Here is the full mental model in one place — the ideas that let you decode any AI headline, ask the sharper question in the room, and make better decisions about where and how to deploy these systems.

The single thread running through everything

AI is software that learns its own rules from examples. Those rules are stored as tuned numbers (parameters). The learning is a repeated loop of guess, measure error, and nudge downhill (gradient descent). The result — at sufficient scale — is systems that can predict the next word so well that fluency, reasoning and hallucination all emerge from the same mechanism. Add the ability to take actions in the world (agentic AI) and the governance questions become as important as the technical ones.

Latent space

The hidden, compressed map of meaning a model builds inside itself — where every concept it has learned becomes a point, and similar things sit close together.

A model can't store the messy, high-dimensional real world directly. So during training it projects everything down into a lower-dimensional space where the patterns that matter are preserved and the noise falls away. "King" lands near "queen"; "Paris" sits to "France" as "Tokyo" sits to "Japan". That internal landscape — invisible, learned, and the source of everything the model can do — is the latent space.

The animation above evokes it: scattered data aggregating into structure, then snapping onto a single learned surface — the logo's own geometry, brought to life.
